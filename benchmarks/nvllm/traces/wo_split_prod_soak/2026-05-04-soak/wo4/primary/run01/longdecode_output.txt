# The Evolution of Transformer Architectures: From Sequence Modeling to Multimodal Intelligence

## 1. Pre-Transformer Sequence Models
Prior to 2017, sequence modeling relied predominantly on Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs) and their variants, Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM) and Gated Recurrent Units (GRU). These architectures processed data sequentially, maintaining a hidden state $h_t$ updated at each time step $t$. While LSTMs mitigated the vanishing gradient problem through gating mechanisms, they suffered from inherent sequential dependencies. Training required processing tokens in order, preventing parallelization across the sequence dimension. Furthermore, long-range dependencies remained difficult to capture due to information compression in the fixed-size hidden state. For example, in machine translation tasks using LSTM-based encoders, the model often struggled to maintain semantic coherence when translating sentences exceeding 50 tokens, as the initial context was diluted by the time the final token was processed.

## 2. The "Attention Is All You Need" Paradigm
The seminal paper "Attention Is All You Need" (Vaswani et al., 2017) introduced the Transformer architecture, discarding recurrence and convolution entirely in favor of self-attention mechanisms. This design allowed for full parallelization of training across sequence positions, drastically reducing training time compared to RNNs. The original architecture utilized an encoder-decoder structure, where the encoder processed the input sequence and the decoder generated the output autoregressively. The model achieved state-of-the-art results on the WMT 2014 English-to-French translation task, surpassing previous hybrid models with significantly fewer training steps. This demonstrated that attention mechanisms alone could capture global dependencies within a sequence without the sequential bottleneck of RNNs.
